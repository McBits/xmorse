XMORSE

Learn Morse code and practice your skills.

It can be boring to transcribe random snippets of text from a Morse code training app. Once you can "read" or "copy" (i.e. transcribe) at a comfortable pace, chatting allows you to hone your skills by chatting with other users who are also learning along with you.

This site does not fully simulate a Morse code key. Instead, the goal is to make it as easy as possible to pick up the code.

To transmit your own messages, press the space bar or click/tap on the Beep button. It is also possible to transmit by clicking on characters in the "cheat sheet", but you should only use this feature sparingly. Remember, the point is to learn!

But why?

In emergency and survival scenarios, basic CW transmissions can be the simplest and most efficient means of radio communication. And Morse code can also be used in other types of communication. (Think: Smoke signals!)

Did you know: It's actually not uncommon for video games and movies to have "easter eggs" based on Morse code!

Although there is no longer a Morse code requirement for amateur radio licensing in the USA and many other countries, it's still a good idea to learn a minimum of 5 WPM. Even better to reach 15-20 WPM, which is pretty typical for CW communications worldwide. Morse code is a nice skill to have, even if you never operate a radio, although I hope spreading the knowledge of the code will inspire more people to get licensed and get on the air.

Once you have your license and a real radio, knowing Morse code will help you potentially reach millions of people around the world. Morse code can be sent and received over channels that are much too noisy for voice communications, and you won't need the sophisticated gear involved with encoding/decoding data transmissions.

Learning the code

How long does it take to learn Morse code? Many people pick up the basic letters, numbers, and common punctuation within just a few hours of practice. However, it can take several weeks or more to reach a speed that allows for comfortable communication. Generally, 15 words per minute is a good target to aim for. After that, you can gradually practice to build your speed up to 20 or 30 WPM. (People who copy above 30 WPM are comparatively rare, but then it's always a treat to connect with someone who can keep up!)

Just keep listing at a speed that's slightly too fast to keep up. Copy as much as you can, always keeping in mind that you won't capture everything, and that's okay. Once you start getting mentally fatigued (perhaps 15 to 45 minutes), reduce the speed a notch and practice for a few minutes at a more manageable speed.

As soon as you have the characters committed to memory, start trying to recognize pairs, triplets, and even whole words. This is essential for reaching higher speeds. There is a point around 15 to 20 WPM where it becomes impossible for your brain to decode Morse code character-by-character. Rather, you will find yourself keeping a small buffer of characters in your head, clearing that buffer in small batches at regular intervals. Challenge yourself!

When you are first beginning, it can help to count the dits and dahs at very low speeds, form a mental image and then look up the letter in your mental lookup table. Try to break this habit as soon as possible.
